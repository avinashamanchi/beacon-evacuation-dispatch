# BEACON film — narration script

Timecodes are from **film 0:00** — the moment the 3-2-1 countdown ends.
Pace: deliberate, ~2.3 words/second. The em-dashes are breaths. Press `N`
during the film for the built-in teleprompter showing these same lines in sync.

Rule of the read: the evidence is spoken quietly. The product is spoken
plainly. Never sell during the first nineteen seconds.

---

## Act I — Evidence (0:00–0:19) · quiet, even

**0:01** — Sixteen of the nineteen people who died in the Eaton fire were
disabled, elderly, or had limited mobility.

**0:07** — The first evacuation order didn't come for more than nine hours.

**0:12** — One man called 911 three times. Calmly. He gave his address. He
said there were two disabled people in the house.

**0:17** — The right help never came.

## Act II — Problem (0:19–0:33)

**0:20** — When an evacuation order goes out, the support line floods.
Every message sounds urgent — and systems that rank by urgency serve the
loudest first.

**0:28** — But tone is not need. The loudest message here is a billing
question. The quietest is two people who cannot leave.

## Act III — How it works (0:33–0:43)

**0:34** — So BEACON never lets the AI decide. The model extracts facts —
twenty-five lines of deterministic Python make the call. "People with
disabilities need more time to evacuate" isn't a finding on a slide here.
It's a variable.

## Act IV — Live (0:43–1:57)

**0:44** — This is the live product. Real tickets, real routing — no mockups.

**0:49** — Thirty messages hit the queue. Watch the gray feed absorb the flood.

**0:56** — The loudest message in the pile: a deductible question, in all
caps. Answered from the Zendesk knowledge base in under a second. No human
ever sees it.

**1:04** — Now the calm one. "My son and I both use wheelchairs. The house
next door is on fire." No exclamation marks.

**1:09** — Fire arrives in eighteen minutes. A wheelchair evacuation takes
forty-five. Minus twenty-seven — they will not make it out alone. So BEACON
sends a lift-equipped van, not a fire engine. The right asset.

**1:17** — "Road out of Miner's Bend is blocked by flames." Different need —
different team. Fire and rescue.

**1:24** — And in Spanish — *estamos atrapados* — because the people most
likely to be missed don't always panic in English.

**1:31** — The fire advances. Every open equation recomputes, live.

**1:38** — Here's the whole argument in one chart: how loud each message
sounds, against how urgent it actually is. The flood lands top-left — loud,
and fine. The wheelchair user is alone in the bottom-right — calm, and
critical. BEACON routes on the horizontal axis only.

**1:47** — And every dispatch prints a receipt. The facts the model
extracted. The rule that fired. The equation, with live values. The crew
that was paged. The AI never decides who gets rescued — and this is the proof.

## Act V — Close (1:57–2:05)

**1:58** — Routed on what you need. Not how you sound. Zendesk Support,
Guide, and Side Conversations. Fifty-one tests passing. Zero network needed
to demo.

**2:03** — The finding is from the Eaton fire. The town on this screen is
fictional. *(beat)* The problem is not.

### Recording with voiceover

Three options, best first:

- **AI track, auto-synced (`V`):** an AI-narrated read (Callum voice) lives at
  `static/vo/beacon-narration.wav`. Press `V` in the film and it plays
  **segment-by-segment** — each beat starts at its film cue and pauses at the
  next real silence (`static/vo-cues.json`, generated by scanning the PCM),
  so it can never drift. Note: it reads a condensed ~74s cut of this script,
  not every word. macOS caveat: `Cmd+Shift+5` records the **mic**, not system
  audio — either let the mic pick up your speakers, or record silent and drop
  the WAV into iMovie.
- **Live read (`N`):** teleprompter on, record with mic, read the strip as it
  changes. The strip is visible in the recording.
- **Two-pass (cleanest):** record the film silent; record this full script as
  audio separately; assemble in iMovie — the timecodes line up 1:1 because
  the film's clock is wall-time locked.
